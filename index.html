Why It Matters

Malaysia's railway mobility system has undergone major transformation over the past decade, shaped by rapid urbanisation, cross-border connectivity, and the disruptive impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.

This visualisation explores how different rail services evolved across Malaysia through ridership trends, recovery trajectories, modal shifts, and metropolitan transfer structures within the national transport network.

SECTION 1 — National Rail Overview

KTMB Monthly Ridership Trends

KTM Komuter consistently records the highest ridership across Malaysia's rail mobility system, while ETS and regional services demonstrate stronger post-pandemic recovery and long-term passenger growth trends.

Rail Service Composition Over Time

KTM Komuter remains the dominant mobility service, while ETS and regional rail systems gradually expand their proportional contribution after the pandemic recovery period across Malaysia's railway network.

SECTION 2 — Comparing Rail Services

Peak Ridership Comparison

KTM Komuter overwhelmingly dominates Malaysia's railway mobility demand, recording peak ridership levels far above regional, cross-border, and long-distance rail services across the national transport system.

Daily Ridership Intensity Patterns(2025)

Weekday commuting demand remains consistently dominant across the calendar, 11 February 2025 recording the highest daily ridership intensity throughout Malaysia's railway network.

Fastest Growing Rail Services

Cross-border rail mobility demonstrates the strongest post-pandemic growth, while several traditional railway services continue experiencing declining passenger demand across Malaysia's evolving transport network.

SECTION 3 — Pandemic and Recovery

Pandemic Collapse and Recovery

Malaysian railway mobility experienced a severe pandemic collapse before entering a rapid recovery phase, with passenger demand stabilising at significantly higher ridership levels after nationwide transport normalization.

Early vs Post Pandemic Modal Shift

Shuttle Tebrau and ETS recorded the strongest post-pandemic mobility recovery between 2022 and 2024, while traditional intercity rail services experienced comparatively slower ridership growth across Malaysia's railway network.

Urban Rail Mobility Expansion

2019

In 2019, Malaysia's urban rail mobility was concentrated around a few dominant corridors. By 2024, the network became more diversified, driven by MRT expansion and the growing role of commuter rail services.

SECTION 4 — National Rail Structure

Major interchange stations remain concentrated around Kuala Lumpur's urban core, highlighting the structural importance of transfer connectivity within Malaysia's metropolitan railway mobility network.

Cross-Border Mobility Corridor

The Johor–Singapore Shuttle Tebrau corridor demonstrates highly directional cross-border commuter mobility, with passenger flows strongly concentrated between JB Sentral and Woodlands CIQ as a critical international railway gateway linking Malaysia and Singapore.

National Rail Network Structure

Malaysia’s railway system is organised around several highly connected urban and intercity corridors, where transfer hubs and major rail lines collectively shape the national mobility structure and strengthen regional transport connectivity.

SECTION 5 — Spatial Mobility Inequality

Labour Force Concentration and Rail Mobility Dependence

Labour force activity remains highly concentrated within Selangor, Kuala Lumpur, and Johor, reinforcing the spatial dependence of Malaysia’s commuter mobility system on economically dominant urban corridors and metropolitan transportation infrastructure.
